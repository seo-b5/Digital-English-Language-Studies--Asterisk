1. Introduction

Studying Latin and Greek plural forms in English is important because these ancient languages are the foundation of Western academic writing[cite: 5, 6]. Over 60% of English words come from Latin and Greek[cite: 7]. Since many English words—especially in science, medicine, technology, and law—come from these roots, knowing their plural rules helps us communicate more clearly in professional jobs[cite: 8].

In this report, our team looks at which nouns are changing to regular English plurals and which ones still use their old forms[cite: 9]. We want to explain why this happens by looking at how these words are used in different subjects and over long periods of time[cite: 10]. Finally, we will use our data to predict which plural forms will become the standard by the year 2040[cite: 11].

2. Research Questions

  • Which nouns are shifting toward regularized English plurals (for example, -s or -es), and which retain their classical forms? [cite: 13]
  • Why does this difference occur? [cite: 14]
  • What patterns emerge when usage data is examined across registers, disciplines, and time periods? [cite: 15]
  • Which plural forms are most likely to be treated as standard by 2040? [cite: 16]

Latin-derived plural forms are consistently maintained, particularly in specialized or academic contexts[cite: 20].

4. Rate and Direction Comparison

The rate of change varies; nouns like "phenomena" show almost no change, while "formulas" and "stadiums" shift rapidly toward English forms[cite: 147, 148]. Specialization and everyday usability are key factors in this variation[cite: 159, 166].

5. 2040 Usage Projections

By 2040, most nouns will likely shift toward regular English forms, except for specialized scientific terms like "phenomena" and "larvae" which will remain stable[cite: 178, 179, 185].

6. Recommendations

Dictionaries should clearly label usage contexts, and education should prioritize English-style plurals as the default[cite: 187, 188].

7. Conclusion

Plural usage is becoming regularized in most cases, but Latin forms are firmly retained in specialized academic and scientific fields to ensure precision[cite: 195].
